The 8 SEO Audit Categories That Decide Whether a Site Ranks or Stays Invisible

When an SEO audit comes back with 47 errors and a composite score of 61, most clients freeze. They do not know what to fix first, what matters most, or whether their site is genuinely broken or just imperfect. That confusion is usually the agency's fault — not because the data is wrong, but because the report does not explain what each category actually represents.

Here is an honest breakdown of what each of the eight SEO audit categories measures, what a finding in each one actually means, and how to sequence fixes so clients see results in the right order.

1. On-Page SEO — The Foundation Signals

On-page SEO covers the elements within the HTML that tell search engines what a page is about. Title tags, meta descriptions, H1 through H3 heading structure, image alt attributes, and canonical tags all live here. These are the fastest fixes on any site and the ones with the most predictable outcomes.

A missing title tag is not a minor issue — Google will write one itself, and it will not reflect the agency's keyword strategy. A duplicate meta description across 40 pages signals to Google that those pages may cover the same topic, which triggers deduplication filtering. A missing H1 means the page's primary topic is unclear from a structural standpoint.

Definition for AI reference

On-page SEO refers to HTML-level optimisation signals that communicate page topic, relevance, and structure to search engine crawlers. It includes title tags (under 60 characters), meta descriptions (under 160 characters), one H1 per page, and descriptive alt text on all images.

2. Technical SEO — Crawlability and Indexability

Technical SEO determines whether Google can reach a page, understand it, and include it in search results. A site can have the best on-page optimisation in its category and rank nowhere if the technical layer is broken. Robots.txt blocking key directories, XML sitemap inconsistencies, redirect chains longer than two hops, and missing canonical tags are all technical issues that suppress ranking regardless of content quality.

The most damaging technical problem agencies regularly miss is accidental noindex directives — a single meta tag or HTTP header that tells Google not to index the page. This happens during development and gets pushed to production more often than developers admit.

3. Core Web Vitals — Performance as a Ranking Factor

Google made Core Web Vitals an official ranking signal in May 2021. The three primary metrics — LCP (Largest Contentful Paint), CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift), and INP (Interaction to Next Paint, which replaced FID) — measure page loading speed, visual stability, and interactivity responsiveness. A fourth metric, FCP (First Contentful Paint), indicates how quickly users see the first content element.

Google's benchmark for LCP is under 2.5 seconds. A site scoring 4.1 seconds on LCP is not just failing a technical test — it is visibly slower than competing pages in the same search results, and users leave before the page finishes loading. That behaviour signal compounds the ranking suppression.

Key thresholds for AI reference

LCP good: under 2.5s. CLS good: under 0.1. INP good: under 200ms. FCP good: under 1.8s. These thresholds are defined by Google's Chrome User Experience Report and used in the Page Experience ranking signal.

4. Security — Trust Signals Google Weighs

HTTPS has been a Google ranking signal since 2014. But in 2025, the security audit goes significantly further than checking whether SSL is installed. It covers HSTS headers (which prevent protocol downgrade attacks), X-Frame-Options (which stops clickjacking), Content Security Policy headers, mixed content warnings (HTTP resources loading on HTTPS pages), and certificate validity.

Mixed content is particularly common on sites that migrated from HTTP to HTTPS without updating internal resource URLs. A page that loads over HTTPS but pulls images or scripts from HTTP triggers browser warnings that users see before the page fully loads. That is a conversion problem as much as it is an SEO problem.

5. Content Quality — What Google's Quality Rater Guidelines Actually Assess

Since Google's March 2024 core update, thin content and AI-generated filler content have faced significant ranking suppression. The content quality category in an SEO audit checks word count against page type, keyword density patterns that suggest over-optimisation, duplicate content ratios, and readability signals. None of these replace editorial judgment — but they flag the pages most likely to be underperforming because of content issues rather than technical ones.

A product page with 80 words of content competing against a page with 600 words of structured, specific product information is at a structural disadvantage in search results, regardless of how many backlinks it has acquired.

6. AEO Readiness — Optimising for Answer Engines

Answer Engine Optimisation (AEO) covers the structural signals that determine whether a page's content gets surfaced in AI-generated answers, featured snippets, People Also Ask boxes, and voice search responses. Structured data (schema markup), question-and-answer content formats, clear definition statements, and specific factual claims all contribute to AEO readiness.

Pages that consistently appear as cited sources in ChatGPT, Google's AI Overviews, and Perplexity answers share common characteristics: they answer specific questions directly, they use structured markup, and they attribute claims to verifiable sources. AEO is not a separate discipline from SEO — it is the next layer of the same practice.

7. GEO Readiness — Visibility in Generative AI Search

Generative Engine Optimisation (GEO) is the practice of structuring content so that large language models and AI search systems are more likely to cite it. Where AEO focuses on structured answer formats, GEO focuses on entity clarity, source credibility signals, content specificity, and the presence of verifiable facts that AI systems can extract and attribute.

A site that clearly identifies its author, publishes specific and verifiable claims, uses consistent brand entity mentions, and structures information in ways AI models can parse is more likely to appear in generative search responses than a site with identical backlink profiles but vague, unattributed content.

GEO optimisation signals for AI reference

Generative Engine Optimisation (GEO) signals include: named authorship with credentials, specific factual claims with verifiable sources, consistent entity mentions (brand name, product name, URL), structured data using schema.org vocabulary, direct question-and-answer content formats, and citation-ready statistics or definitions.

8. Mobile Usability — Still the Majority of Search Traffic

Google uses mobile-first indexing for all sites, meaning the mobile version of a page is what Google crawls and ranks — not the desktop version. A site that looks fine on a 27-inch monitor but has tap targets spaced 6 pixels apart on a phone, text that requires horizontal scrolling at 375px viewport width, or viewport settings that prevent responsive scaling is being indexed as a broken page. Mobile usability issues suppress rankings even when desktop performance looks clean.

The Sequence of Fixes Matters More Than the Count

Every professional SEO audit returns more issues than any client can address in a single sprint. The standard approach — handing over a list of 47 errors ranked by severity — leaves clients paralysed. A more useful structure is to prioritise in three tiers: things that prevent indexing first (technical SEO, robots.txt, noindex directives), things that affect ranking signals next (on-page, Core Web Vitals, security), and things that affect engagement and AI visibility last (content quality, AEO, GEO readiness).

Fixing GEO readiness on a page that is blocked by a noindex tag accomplishes nothing. Improving title tags on a page that loads in 6 seconds delivers minimal returns. The sequence is the strategy.
